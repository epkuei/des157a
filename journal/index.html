Visual Thinking Analysis

17 May 2026

At first glance, this image appears to be a simple nature photograph, but the photographer's deliberate choices reveal something far more intentional. The camera is placed almost flush with the ground, making the shadowed pavement dominate the foreground while the golden-leaved tree blazes against a vivid teal sky. The converging tile lines function as a subtle leading line, drawing the eye upward and giving the image an almost reverent quality. The contrast in color temperature, cool cinematic blue against warm amber, feels emotionally honest rather than artificial. To push this further, I would suggest eliminating the utility poles on the right edge and shooting closer to golden hour to make the tree's glow feel even more otherworldly.

This image captures a performer seated on a mossy stone platform, dressed in a flowing white gown, within a stage environment designed to resemble an otherworldly forest or underwater dreamscape. Massive translucent curtains billow behind her in deep teal-blue, while scattered amber sparks float in the background like fireflies, and a partially visible shipwreck prop sits quietly in shadow at the right edge. What you would not know from looking alone is how the grandeur of the stage was engineered to make the entire audience feel submerged, as being there in person meant the sound filled the room in a way the photograph can only gesture toward. This image fits into a concert archive about live music as an emotional and aesthetic experience, evidence that some performances are closer to theater or visual art than a typical show. It reflects something about me too, that I seek out artists who treat their concerts as immersive worlds, and that I am drawn to moments of intimacy within massive, collective spaces.

Visual Thinking Strategies Research

12 May 2026

Images operate on multiple levels at once. On the surface, a photo shows you what something looks like, but underneath, things like color, light exposure, framing, and texture are quietly doing a lot of work to shape how you feel about what you're seeing. That's the core idea behind the NYT article's approach to close visual reading, and it's something I noticed almost immediately when exploring the Marvell Tile & Stone website.

Marvell is a Perth-based tiling and natural stone company, and their site does something I really appreciated: the material itself becomes the design. Rather than using generic backgrounds or abstract layouts, the site leans into tile and stone textures as the visual foundation, then layers the project photography on top. It makes the whole experience feel cohesive and purposeful, like the medium and the message are the same thing. The warm, muted color palette (creamy off-whites and deep earthy browns) reinforces a sense of quality and calm without needing to say anything explicitly.

What's interesting from a visual thinking perspective is that nothing on this site feels accidental. The imagery isn't just "nice photos of rooms," the varying angles, the close-up texture shots, the careful lighting, all of it builds an argument for craftsmanship before you read a single word. That's what visual thinking is really about: recognizing that images aren't just decoration, they're communication. A drawback might be that the minimal text leaves some users wanting more context, but for the audience it's targeting, the images say enough.

Overlay Design Pattern Research

26 April 2026

Reading Best Practices for Modals/Overlays/Dialog Windowsby Naema Baskanderi, my underestanding of overlay designs has expanded. The article defines a modal as an element that sits on top of an application's main window, disabling the parent page but keeping it visible so users must interact with the modal before they can return. The best way to use an overlay, according to Baskanderi, is with the intention to grab a user's attention, collect necessary input, or show additional information in context.

The article also breaks down the anatomy of a well-designed modal, highlighting several key components. The most important feature that an overlay should have is an escape hatch (a cancellation button of sorts). The escape hatch acts as a way for users to leave the overlay, typically connected to a user's escape key as well. She also notes that modals should not exceed 50% of the screen, and that keyboard focus should move into the modal when it opens and be trapped there until it is closed, a key to making accessible designs.

Since I have not yet had the opportunity to design or implement an overlay in my own work, I found this article especially valuable as a foundation. Knowing the best practices behind this feature before using it means I can approach it thoughtfully rather than learning through trial and error — and I look forward to applying these guidelines when the opportunity arises.

Form Design Research

13 April 2026

After reading the article Best Practices for Form Design by Salim Ansari, my knowledge and practices in UX design have been reinforced. The article emphasizes best practices in UX design that boost engagement and retention, and these should be applied to both mobile and desktop applications. These different components and design forms are not just suggestions or recommendations; they should be applied in the design to reduce user frustration.

The article specifically mentions components necessary when designs require users to fill out forms, such as an onboarding form to join the application's database. Here, Ansari discusses various components, such as a multi-step progress bar to help users visualize how far they are in the form, labels to indicate where information must be entered, placeholder text to guide users, and more.

In Spring Quarter 2025, I participated in a 6-week conceptual design sprint. During this sprint, my team and I were tasked to create a mobile application for families to track their histories and store memorabilia. During this process, we integrated many of the design practices suggested by Ansari. One major design practice we integrated and iterated on was the multi-step progress bar to help users visualize how many steps of the form remained. We experimented with presenting this information in two ways: the first was a single line in a lighter grey that would fill with the themed application color, and the second was a bubble format with three main steps that would fill in as the user continued through the form. Ultimately, we decided to use the former, the continuous line, as there were steps within steps, and users felt more motivated to continue as they saw continuous progress.
